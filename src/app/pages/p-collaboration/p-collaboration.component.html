Project Collaboration Tool

A project creation application to encourage collaboration on community development challenges

UX Designer

Framing the Problem

There are a variety of foreign development organizations that work in the international development space in a certain capacity. There are Service-Education organization that develop educational summer programs that bring students from the US to do service projects in Latin-American and Caribbean communities. There are foundations that conduct medical missions by surveying the medical problems of a community for a week or two searching for patients, and then treat those patients with pharmaceuticals, medical procedures, or surgeries. And there are a variety of development organizations that work in grassroots (bottom-up) development such as Peace Corps or bureaucratic (top-down) development such as United States Agency for International Development (USAID).

These organizations address international development and humanitarian efforts in a variety of ways but could be more efficient. We’re designing a platform to facilitate easier collaboration amongst these organizations.

CHALLENGE

Project Collaboration for Nonprofits is Hard

Nonprofit resources aren’t being used efficiently due to less collaboration. This makes it so that people who are in need don’t get help.

SOLUTION

A collaborative platform designed for humanitarian work

I designed a platform to facilitate the creation of a project collaboratively, making it easier to work on the same project amongst multiple organizations.

Key Takeaways

Designing for nonprofit work is very different than designing for for-profit ventures

One big misconception was to “beat” what the other nonprofit (and tech) solutions on the market were doing which actually impeded how I should design the project. By focusing on all the features other competitors had, I quickly realized that I would be just designing a “better” version of something that already exists and that doesn’t teach me anything. By throwing out the concept that I had to focus on software features and the “what”, I decided to focus on the “why”. Why did people like Slack and Trello and how can I transform that experience to accomplish goals within international development.

Focus on one great feature and iterate

One big change I had to make was where to limit the program/project creation tool. The United Nations provided a framework for what our goals should be, which targets to hit within that goal, and how to measure progress to prove that work was done within our goals/targets. They don’t provide a framework to manage on-the-ground operations. Through personal experience and user research, I quickly realized that there ere way too many variables regarding management on the ground. With so many development strategies regarded as the “right” way to do development, I removed a feature that allowed you to manage the program after project creation, focusing on providing a framework to manage only the creation of projects. I also quickly realized that designing any sort of in-house chat room made less sense now. With chat solutions so plentiful, it made no sense to add another interface for people to have to check.

Relax

Along every step of the way, I was always trying to find that idea that clicked and would solve all the pain-points I discovered in international civic tech. Not only was what ridiculous, it hindered me from thinking outside the box sometimes! This is something I'm beginning to realize as the software developer part of me is learning to accept the design process!
